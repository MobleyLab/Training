# Track Changes / Suggesting Mode

How we use it for paper revision/editing:

1. Always have Track Changes (Overleaf) / Suggesting mode (Google Docs) turned on before you edit. If it's off, your edits are invisible and unreviewable — I have to assume you made none.
2. Accept my changes as you read them — don't leave my edits sitting untracked. If you disagree with one, don't just accept it: edit it further (or reject it if it's completely wrong) and leave a comment saying why.
3. Leave your own changes tracked. Don't accept your own edits. The whole point: if your edits are tracked, I only have to review what's new. If you accept them yourself, I have to re-read the entire paper to find what changed. See "exceptions" below.
4. Comment on anything substantive — explain why you made the change, not just what it is. Typo fixes and formatting don't need a comment; anything that changes meaning, structure, or an argument does.
5. Resolution is reciprocal, and only the author resolves:
   * I resolve your comments, once I've checked your revision.
   * You resolve my comments, once you've addressed them.
   * Never resolve someone else's comment on their behalf, even if you think it's handled — flag it as done in a reply instead and let them close it.
6. Never leave comments unanswered. If I see I left a comment and it has no answer, I have to assume you didn't see it or address it. If you disagree or have questions, ask. If you addressed, note that you did.

Why this matters: tracked, commented changes mean I only ever read the revisions, so I move way faster. Untracked or unexplained edits mean re-reading the whole document every pass, which is slower for both of us and more likely to introduce silent errors.

## Exceptions

- If edits are format-only (inserting whitespace, etc.) or fix typos or obvious grammar errors, no tracking is needed, just accept the changes to reduce the amount of review needed.
- If I leave a comment that explains a general principle to the author (like "Always define/explain technical terms when you first introduce them") rather than suggesting a specific edit, mark the comment as resolved once you're done with it.
